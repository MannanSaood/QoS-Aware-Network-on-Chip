1. Introduction
The proliferation of many-core processor architectures has made the Network-on-Chip (NoC) the
primary on-chip communication backbone for modern System-on-Chip (SoC) designs. In systems
comprising tens to hundreds of processing cores, the NoC fabric must simultaneously carry
heterogeneous traffic classes — coherence request/response messages, bulk memory transfers, and
latency-critical real-time data streams — each exhibiting fundamentally different timing and
bandwidth demands. Without Quality of Service (QoS) enforcement at the router level, these
traffic classes compete equally for shared resources, resulting in priority inversion and
unpredictable latency behaviour that violates system-level timing contracts.
Despite significant research into QoS-aware routing algorithms and virtual channel (VC) based
arbitration policies, a critical verification gap persists: the majority of published NoC designs are
validated through functional simulation alone, without a structured, coverage-driven verification
environment that systematically exercises QoS behavioural properties under adversarial multiclass traffic injection patterns. This project addresses this gap through two complementary
contributions. First, a QoS-aware 5-port mesh NoC router is designed in RTL (SystemVerilog),
incorporating two-VC priority separation per input port and a hierarchical priority arbiter that
guarantees high-priority (HP) flit pre-emption over low-priority (LP) flits at every arbitration
cycle. Second, a directed SystemVerilog verification environment is constructed to validate that
defined QoS properties hold under worst-case congestion scenarios, with quantitative latency and
throughput metrics reported per traffic class. The project draws on the architectural precedent
established by the OpenPiton manycore research platform (Princeton University), which
implements a mesh NoC with traffic prioritisation hooks, and extends it with a formally specified
verification methodology targeting QoS correctness.

3. Literature Survey and Review
A thorough survey of 28 journal articles, conference papers, and reference texts was conducted,
spanning on-chip interconnect architecture, QoS arbitration theory, formal verification
methodology, and modern NoC benchmarking frameworks. The primary sources drawn upon
are IEEE Transactions on Computers, IEEE Transactions on VLSI Systems, ACM ASPLOS,
DAC, and MICRO proceedings. Six representative works are summarised below.
[1] W. J. Dally and B. Towles, “Route Packets, Not Wires: On-Chip Interconnection
Networks,”

DAC,

2001.

This foundational work formalised the concept of NoC as a structured interconnect paradigm.
The authors proposed virtual channel flow control as the primary mechanism for deadlock
avoidance, introduced wormhole switching for low-latency flit routing, and analysed the
throughput-latency relationship under uniform random traffic. This paper directly informs the
VC-based priority architecture adopted in our design. [2] E. Bolotin, I. Cidon, R. Ginosar, and
A. Kolodny, “QNoC: QoS Architecture and Design Process for Network on Chip,” Journal of
Systems Architecture, vol. 50, no. 2–3, 2004. QNoC introduced a formal QoS service model
for NoC routers, defining guaranteed throughput (GT) and best-effort (BE) traffic classes with
separate virtual channel pools. The authors demonstrated priority-based arbitration between
GT and BE flits, showing measurable latency reduction for GT traffic under congestion. This
paper is the closest methodological precedent for our two-VC priority arbiter design. [3] N.
Agarwal et al., “GARNET: A Detailed On-Chip Network Model inside a Full-System
Simulator,” ISPASS, 2009. GARNET provides a cycle-accurate NoC simulation framework
integrated with gem5, modelling per-VC buffers, credit-based flow control, and arbitration
logic. The paper validates its model against RTL implementations, establishing a performance
baseline methodology. We adopt GARNET’s traffic injection and latency measurement
conventions for our testbench metrics. [4] J. Balkind et al., “OpenPiton: An Open Source
Manycore Research Framework,” ASPLOS, 2016. OpenPiton implements a scalable tiled
manycore SoC with a mesh NoC featuring arbitration logic and traffic prioritisation hooks for
coherence traffic. While OpenPiton demonstrates the viability of priority-aware NoC routing
in silicon-targeting RTL, it does not provide a structured verification environment targeting

4. Limitations of the Existing Work (Gap Analysis) and Motivation
The literature review reveals four critical gaps that motivate this project. First, existing QoS NoC
architectures (QNoC, GARNET) are evaluated exclusively through simulation-level behavioural
models; no published work presents a coverage-driven RTL verification environment that formally
closes QoS properties against a synthesisable router implementation. Second, the OpenPiton
platform, which is the closest 1:1 architectural precedent for a mesh NoC with priority hooks,
provides no defined test plan, coverage model, or pass/fail criteria for QoS behavioural
correctness. Its arbitration logic has not been subjected to adversarial traffic injection designed to
provoke priority inversion corner cases. Third, most NoC verification literature targets structural
correctness (deadlock-freedom, livelock-freedom) rather than service-level properties (worst-case
HP latency under LP congestion, credit starvation scenarios). The distinction is significant: a router
can be functionally correct under nominal traffic while exhibiting QoS violations under adversarial
injection patterns. Fourth, no open-source, parameterisable RTL QoS NoC router exists with an
accompanying verification environment and documented coverage closure methodology, leaving
a reusability gap for academic and industrial adopters. This project directly addresses all four gaps
by delivering a verified, parameterisable RTL QoS router with a documented, reproducible
verification methodology.

Key Points to Include●

System Architecture: The router consists of five pipeline stages: (1) Flit Buffer with
2-VC input queues per port using credit-based flow control; (2) Route Computation
unit implementing XY routing (East-first, then North); (3) Virtual Channel
Allocator assigning VC0/VC1 per packet header priority bit; (4) Priority Switch
Arbiter implementing fixed-priority round-robin, VC0 always pre-empts VC1 at
arbitration; (5) Crossbar Switch with 5x5 non-blocking interconnect. The testbench
wraps the DUT with a Traffic Generator (configurable injection rate and priority
mix per port), a Scoreboard (checks routing correctness and VC data integrity), and
a Latency Monitor (records per-VC timestamp delta from injection to ejection).

9.1 Annexure -1 Definition of Prototype or Proof-Of-Concept

A Prototype / Proof-of-Concept (PoC) in the 6th semester is an early demonstration of
the technical feasibility of the chosen solution, without aiming for full functionality or
production-level quality.
A valid PoC must satisfy the followingA. Demonstrates Core Feasibility
The PoC must implement one or more core functional requirements to prove that
the proposed approach, technology stack, and architecture are viable.
B. Limited Scope, Not Complete Coverage
The PoC is not expected to implement all functional or any of non-functional
requirements; it may use assumptions, mock data, simplified workflows, or partial
integrations.
C. Exploratory by Design
The PoC is intended to validate assumptions, identify risks, and surface technical
challenges, even if parts of the solution fail or need redesign.
D. Architecture-Guiding Artifact
Successful PoC implementation and demonstration should influence the final
system architecture, technology choices, and requirement refinement in the 7th
semester.
E. Clearly Distinguishable from the Final Project
The PoC prioritizes learning and feasibility over robustness, optimization,
scalability, security, or polish, all of which are addressed systematically in later
semesters.

9.2 Annexure -2 Definition of Functional Requirements
What Are Functional Requirements? Functional Requirements describe WHAT your system
must do. They define the features, behaviors, and capabilities that your prototype or proof-ofconcept must demonstrate. Examples: • A user can log in • System captures sensor data • Model
classifies images • Dashboard displays results
Functional Requirements must be SMART compliant. Writing Good Functional Requirements
-> {Actor (Who is the User?) + Action (what action?) + System Response (expected response
from the system)}. Avoid mentioning underlying technology details in functional requirements.
Example: The system shall allow users to reset their password via email verification.

Each requirement should be ●

S – Specific → Clearly defined

●

M – Measurable → Can be validated/tested

●

A – Achievable → Realistic for prototype

●

R – Relevant → Directly tied to problem

●

T – Time-Bound → Feasible within Semester VI
